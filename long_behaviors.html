Scene-Conditioned Maritime Pattern-of-Life · Long-Timescale Arm · Cold-Start

Multi-hour behaviors, recognized in a port never trained on

Feasibility for the long-timescale half of a multi-method PoL architecture (gPC continuous dynamics at short timescales; transformers over discretized trajectories at long timescales). A 256-step, 2-minute-token transformer (~8.5 h of context) was trained with 16–64-minute span masking on Puget Sound + SF Bay, then applied to Chesapeake windows it never saw. Its window embeddings, clustered on the training ports only, land on nameable multi-hour behaviors — stop-and-go pot/trap tending, slow area-working (fishing-enriched 2.5×), shoreline passage (fishing-enriched 3.8×), directed transit — and the same clusters reappear with matching signatures in the new region. The context experiment splits cleanly: generic underway motion saturates at ~1.6 h of history, but rhythmic work behaviors keep improving all the way to 6.7 h — long context pays exactly where behavior has long structure.

Does long context actually help? (cold-start)

Predicting the same masked 30-minute span in unseen Chesapeake, varying how much history the model may attend to. Falling NLL = attention exploits multi-hour structure, in a region with zero training data.

Discovered behavior classes — Chesapeake, assigned cold

Clusters were fit on Puget+SF embeddings only. Toggle classes on the map; hover a track. Consistency = cosine similarity between a cluster's kinematic signature on the training ports vs Chesapeake (1.0 = the behavior means the same thing in the new region).

Read this with the v1/v2 demos: the short-timescale arm (per-minute causal scoring, live display) judges moment-to-moment motion given place; this long arm recognizes what a vessel has been doing for hours — the level where "movement consistent with fishing" or "tending a pot line" lives. Both are trained once, carry no regional database, and transfer cold.
Feature-following (the known gap), measured — two ablations. The scene stream says "what is the water right here", so turns mandated by a bending coastline or channel are not predictable from the inputs. Two fixes were trained head-to-head against the plain model, all from the same static raster. (1) A look-ahead token (what water is ~1.5 km ahead on course: deep / shallow / land-blocked) improves turn prediction exactly where hypothesized — turns in constrained water: +1.6% NLL — and nowhere else. (2) A first directional variant (5 probes at ±60°, encoding which side has more water) did not beat it on turns (+0.5%), though it helps predict staying straight in constrained water (+1.5%): "water symmetric ahead" is evidence no turn is coming. Honest read: the simple look-ahead wins for now; the directional signal is corrupted by course noise and doesn't encode turn timing. Properly-motivated next steps: shore-tangent alignment (is the vessel's course parallel to the local coastline?), then charted-channel tokens and egocentric scene crops (Route B).
